    Anaphylaxis

    Life-Threatening Emergency

    Anaphylaxis is a severe, life-threatening allergic reaction. Intramuscular adrenaline is the ONLY first-line treatment - give immediately if suspected. Do not delay for antihistamines or steroids. When in doubt, give adrenaline.

    Recognition

    Diagnosis Criteria

    Anaphylaxis is likely when all 3 criteria are met:

    1. Sudden onset and rapid progression of symptoms
    2. Life-threatening Airway and/or Breathing and/or Circulation problems
    3. Skin and/or mucosal changes (flushing, urticaria, angioedema)

    Additional Features

    • Exposure to known allergen supports diagnosis but is NOT required
    • Skin changes alone are NOT a sign of anaphylaxis
    • Skin changes can be subtle or absent in up to 20% of reactions

    Common Triggers

    • Foods (nuts, shellfish, eggs, milk)
    • Insect stings (bee, wasp)
    • Medications (antibiotics, NSAIDs, contrast)
    • Latex

    Emergency Treatment Guide

    Open Treatment Guide in new tab

    Key Treatment Points

    Immediate Management

    1. Remove trigger if possible (stop infusion, remove stinger)
    2. Call for help - senior support
    3. Adrenaline IM - anterolateral thigh (vastus lateralis)
      • Adult: 500 micrograms (0.5mL of 1:1000)
      • Child >12 years: 500 micrograms (0.5mL of 1:1000)
      • Child 6-12 years: 300 micrograms (0.3mL of 1:1000)
      • Child <6 years: 150 micrograms (0.15mL of 1:1000)
    4. Position patient - lie flat with legs elevated (or comfortable if breathing difficult)
    5. High-flow oxygen - 15L/min via non-rebreather mask
    6. IV access and fluid resuscitation if shocked
    7. Repeat adrenaline every 5 minutes if no improvement

    Second-Line Treatments

    After adrenaline and resuscitation:

    • Antihistamine (chlorphenamine not available in NZ):
      • Cetirizine (oral - as per ALS guideline):
        • Adult/child >12 years: 10mg
        • Child 6-12 years: 5mg
        • Child 2-6 years: 2.5mg
      • Fexofenadine (oral alternative):
        • Adult/child >12 years: 180mg
        • Child 6-12 years: 120mg
    • Corticosteroid: Hydrocortisone (IM or slow IV)
      • Adult/child >12 years: 200mg
      • Child 6-12 years: 100mg
      • Child 1-6 years: 50mg

    Disposition

    All Patients Require Observation

    Observation time depends on severity and response to treatment

    Fast-Track Discharge (2 hours observation)

    May consider after 2 hours from resolution if ALL of the following:

    • Good response (within 5-10 minutes) to a single dose of adrenaline given within 30 minutes of onset
    • Complete resolution of symptoms
    • Patient already has unused adrenaline auto-injectors and has been trained how to use them
    • Adequate supervision following discharge

    Standard Observation (Minimum 6 hours)

    Recommended if:

    • 2 doses of IM adrenaline needed to treat reaction
    • Previous biphasic reaction

    Extended Observation (Minimum 12 hours)

    Required if any one of the following:

    • Severe reaction requiring >2 doses of adrenaline
    • Patient has severe asthma or reaction involved severe respiratory compromise
    • Possibility of continuing absorption of allergen (e.g., slow-release medicines)
    • Patient presents late at night, or may not be able to respond to any deterioration
    • Patient in area where access to emergency care is difficult

    Discharge Planning

    Before Discharge - Essential Actions

    1. Prescribe adrenaline auto-injector (2 devices)
      • Adult/child >30kg: EpiPen® 300 micrograms
      • Child 15-30kg: EpiPen Jr® 150 micrograms
    2. Demonstrate how to use auto-injector
    3. Written information on anaphylaxis and management
    4. Allergy action plan - what to do if reaction occurs
    5. Follow-up arranged with GP or allergy clinic
    6. Medical alert bracelet recommended
    7. Avoid identified trigger - clear written advice
